# Qualitative Scene Review

Use this reference after deterministic checks. The goal is not "pretty text"; the goal is a playable scene where each choice reveals character, pressure, and consequence.

## Core Questions

- What is the scene testing: compassion, prudence, ambition, trust, fear, obligation, reputation, scarcity, pride, honesty?
- What specific pressure makes the decision hard right now?
- What does each involved person want, and what are they unwilling to say directly?
- What truth changes between the start beat and each ending?
- Why would a player choose each option for a reason other than min-maxing?

## Choice Quality

Strong choices:

- express different values, not just different amounts of kindness;
- each carry a benefit and a cost;
- make the player choose a posture toward a person, rule, scarcity, or risk;
- have labels that are verbs or decisions, not vague moods;
- have descriptions that reveal trade-off or intent;
- avoid one obviously correct "designer favorite" answer.

Weak choices:

- are pure good / pure bad / pure neutral;
- only differ by numeric reward;
- promise consequences the effects do not support;
- have labels that are too abstract to picture;
- restate the same moral axis three times.

## Beat Quality

Strong beats:

- add one new piece of pressure, evidence, or emotional information;
- let the situation turn before the ending;
- use concrete details from the room, object, customer, apprentice, shop, weather, money, stock, or body language;
- avoid explaining the theme in narrator language.

A beat path with only one beat is often too thin because the player chooses before the situation has turned. A path longer than five beats may be too talky unless every beat changes the decision.

## Aftermath Quality

Strong aftermath:

- states what the player did without moralizing;
- shows immediate consequence in the room;
- makes spotlighted property changes feel narratively earned;
- names a future echo that can matter later without inventing cross-scene facts not represented by state;
- preserves ambiguity when the choice is genuinely mixed.

Weak aftermath:

- congratulates or scolds the player;
- says "things changed" without naming how;
- ignores the choice's cost;
- describes a property change that did not happen;
- uses grand future claims the game cannot support.

## Prose Style

Prefer:

- specific nouns and small gestures;
- short sentences mixed with occasional longer cadence;
- implied emotion over labelled emotion;
- shop-sim material reality: coins, stock, bottles, ledgers, time, fatigue, weather, customers, reputation;
- consequences that feel local before they feel symbolic.

Avoid:

- generic cosy warmth with no bite;
- repeated stock phrases across choices;
- "somehow" as a shortcut for unexplained emotional logic;
- abstract theme words replacing action;
- every ending resolving into the same bittersweet tone;
- purple prose that makes the player inspect style instead of consequence.

## Revision Pass

When improving a scene:

1. Name the central dramatic question.
2. Mark each terminal choice as value / cost / benefit / changed state.
3. Delete or rewrite any option that is strictly dominated.
4. Make each beat introduce new information.
5. Tighten labels and descriptions before rewriting long prose.
6. Rewrite aftermath to match effects and spotlight properties.
7. Run the deterministic audit again.
