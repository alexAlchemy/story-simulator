# Potions & Shop Mechanics Wishlist

This document sketches high-level potion and store mechanics that would
complement the narrative goals of `system-cosy-shop`. It is a wishlist, not a
commitment to build every system immediately. The aim is to give the shop layer
enough mechanical crunch that story choices have material texture: scarcity,
quality, preparation, reputation, risk, and tradeoffs.

For a deeper look at how shop, craft, relationship, town, and story growth can
accumulate over time, see [Cosy Shop Progression](./progression.md).

The guiding principle is:

> Shop mechanics should create interesting pressure for authored scenes, not pull
> the game away from character drama.

## Core Shop Loop

The basic loop should be legible and satisfying:

1. **Prepare:** inspect stock, coins, fatigue, open orders, rent pressure, known
   customer needs, and available ingredients.
2. **Choose activity:** open the shop, brew, restock, forage, take commissions,
   train the apprentice, repair the store, pursue town errands, or rest.
3. **Serve customers:** match needs to potions, negotiate price, offer credit,
   refuse unsafe requests, recommend alternatives, or improvise under pressure.
4. **Resolve consequences:** earn coins, spend stock, alter reputation, create or
   clear obligations, change relationships, and reveal follow-up scenes.
5. **Close the day:** pay costs, review unresolved orders, update town rumours,
   process fatigue, and surface tomorrow's pressures.

The player should feel they are running a shop, not merely clicking through
scenes, but the loop should remain lightweight enough that story events can keep
the lead.

## Potion Catalogue

Potions should have enough identity to support choice and consequence.

Useful potion traits:

- **Category:** healing, calming, courage, sleep, clarity, luck, glamour,
  antidote, growth, memory, warmth, warding, or household utility.
- **Potency:** weak, standard, strong, exceptional, unstable.
- **Quality:** flawed, ordinary, fine, masterwork, suspicious.
- **Shelf life:** stable, short-lived, spoiling, volatile.
- **Risk profile:** safe, side-effect prone, addictive, illegal, socially
  sensitive, morally ambiguous.
- **Customer fit:** correct match, partial match, overkill, wrong cure, dangerous
  misuse.
- **Signature:** the shop's recognizable style: gentle, precise, potent,
  beautiful, cheap, experimental, traditional.

The catalogue does not need hundreds of recipes. A smaller set of expressive
potions with clear fictional uses is more valuable than a large flat inventory.

## Ingredients

Ingredients create scarcity, planning, and regional flavour.

Good ingredient mechanics:

- **Common staples:** cheap, reliable ingredients used by many basic recipes.
- **Rare reagents:** limited resources that make powerful or story-significant
  potions possible.
- **Seasonal items:** ingredients that appear only during certain days, weather,
  festivals, or story phases.
- **Perishable goods:** stock that decays or loses quality if ignored.
- **Ethical sources:** ingredients that can be bought cheaply from dubious
  suppliers, gathered responsibly, overharvested, gifted by townsfolk, or earned
  through favours.
- **Substitutions:** imperfect ingredient swaps that alter potency, side effects,
  price, or reputation.

Ingredient scarcity should rarely be a simple "no." It should more often become
"yes, but": weaker potion, higher risk, social debt, extra fatigue, lost profit,
or a later complication.

## Brewing

Brewing should reward preparation and identity without becoming a dense crafting
spreadsheet.

Possible brewing mechanics:

- **Recipe knowledge:** known recipes, half-learned recipes, experimental
  variants, and recipes taught by customers, mentors, books, or the apprentice.
- **Technique checks:** decisions around temperature, timing, order of
  ingredients, dilution, stabilising, bottling, or charmwork.
- **Batch size:** brew one careful dose, a modest batch, or a large batch that
  risks quality loss.
- **Fatigue pressure:** tired brewing increases the chance of flawed potions,
  accidents, wasted stock, or sharp dialogue in related scenes.
- **Apprentice assistance:** the apprentice can speed work, reduce fatigue, learn
  confidence, or make mistakes depending on trust and training.
- **Experimentation:** unknown combinations can discover recipes, produce odd
  side effects, waste rare ingredients, or create future scene hooks.
- **Quality bands:** outcomes should be understandable: flawed, serviceable,
  good, excellent, dangerous.

The ideal brewing system creates a little anticipation before the bell rings:
"I have three good cures, one risky sleep draught, and no antidotes. Please do not
let today be snakebite day."

## Customer Needs

Customers should arrive with practical needs and hidden dramatic layers.

Customer request types:

- **Ordinary need:** a clear request for a common potion.
- **Urgent need:** time pressure, injury, panic, or a customer who cannot wait.
- **Poverty pressure:** the customer needs help but cannot pay full price.
- **Suspicious request:** the potion could be used for harm, deception, or social
  trouble.
- **Status request:** a wealthy or influential customer expects priority,
  discretion, or special treatment.
- **Repeat relationship:** a familiar customer remembers prior generosity,
  refusal, price, quality, or advice.
- **Misdiagnosed need:** the customer asks for one potion but actually needs a
  different intervention.
- **Commission:** a custom order that ties up stock, time, or future obligation.

The mechanics should support both transactional play and human complication. A
customer is not only a wallet; they are a future rumour, ally, burden, rival,
friend, creditor, or regret.

## Pricing & Payment

Price should be a moral and strategic choice.

Possible payment mechanics:

- **Set price:** free, discounted, fair, premium, exploitative.
- **Haggling:** customers may bargain based on urgency, relationship, class,
  reputation, or gossip.
- **Credit:** let someone pay later, creating obligation and possible follow-up.
- **Barter:** accept ingredients, information, labour, protection, recipes, or
  social introductions instead of coins.
- **Charity ledger:** generous acts can build goodwill but weaken short-term
  survival.
- **Debt pressure:** rent, supplier bills, repairs, taxes, fines, or old loans
  make generosity materially costly.
- **Reputation feedback:** high prices may improve wealth but damage goodwill;
  fair treatment may build loyal customers; repeated undercharging may invite
  dependency or admiration.

Pricing should make the player ask: "What is this worth?" and then, more
importantly, "Worth to whom?"

## Inventory & Stock

Inventory should be small enough to understand and varied enough to matter.

Wishlist mechanics:

- **Potion stock:** finished potions ready to sell.
- **Ingredient stock:** raw materials for brewing.
- **Reserved stock:** items promised to commissions or kept for emergencies.
- **Spoilage:** some potions and ingredients degrade over time.
- **Storage quality:** better shelves, wards, cold cabinets, or cellar space
  reduce spoilage and accidents.
- **Shortage states:** being low on key goods changes available scenes and
  customer outcomes.
- **Overstock:** too many unsold goods tie up money and may spoil, but can help
  during rushes.

Inventory pressure should be visible before choices, so failures feel earned
rather than arbitrary.

## Store Upgrades

Upgrades should express the shopkeeper's priorities, not just raise numbers.

Possible upgrade tracks:

- **Brewing equipment:** better cauldrons, alembics, infusers, scales, burners,
  charm-circles, and safety wards.
- **Customer space:** warmer lighting, chairs, privacy screens, waiting shelves,
  tea service, signage, and accessibility improvements.
- **Storage:** larger cabinets, labelled drawers, warded lockboxes, cellar racks,
  drying lines, and refrigeration charms.
- **Apprentice tools:** practice station, notebooks, safety gear, personal work
  bench, or teaching materials.
- **Reputation signals:** signboard, window display, town notices, seals of
  approval, professional memberships.
- **Risky shortcuts:** black-market reagents, aggressive advertising, cheaper
  bottles, unsafe speed-brewing tools.

Upgrades should alter available strategies and scene texture. A privacy screen
might invite secret requests. A training bench might unlock apprentice scenes. A
fancy sign might attract wealthier customers and sharper judgement.

## Reputation & Town Response

The town should respond to patterns, not isolated arithmetic.

Useful reputation channels:

- **Shop standing:** perceived competence and legitimacy.
- **Goodwill:** whether people feel the shop is part of the community.
- **Gossip heat:** how loudly the town is talking about recent events.
- **Specialisation:** what the shop becomes known for: cheap help, precise cures,
  experimental work, compassion, discretion, dangerous potency, or social climbing.
- **Customer memory:** key NPCs remember price, quality, refusal, trust, and
  whether private matters stayed private.
- **Faction pressure:** guilds, healers, suppliers, nobles, clergy, workers,
  farmers, or rival shops may react differently to the same behaviour.

Reputation should affect who arrives, what they ask for, how much they will pay,
how much they trust advice, and what rumours shape the day.

## Apprentice & Labour

The apprentice is both a relationship and a store mechanic.

Possible mechanics:

- **Training:** improves confidence, reliability, recipe knowledge, or customer
  handling.
- **Delegation:** assign brewing, cleaning, front-counter work, errands, sorting
  ingredients, or research.
- **Trust and fear:** influence whether the apprentice admits mistakes, takes
  initiative, asks questions, or hides problems.
- **Mistake handling:** errors can waste stock, harm reputation, reveal teaching
  style, or become bonding moments.
- **Growth path:** the apprentice may become careful, bold, fearful, inventive,
  compassionate, resentful, loyal, or independent.
- **Labour relief:** good teamwork can reduce player fatigue or allow more
  ambitious shop days.

Apprentice mechanics should never reduce the character to a productivity bonus.
Their growth should be one of the clearest ways the shop reflects the player's
values.

## Risk, Accidents & Side Effects

Potions are magical goods. They should sometimes misbehave.

Risk sources:

- tired brewing
- bad substitutions
- unstable ingredients
- rushing an urgent order
- poor storage
- apprentice mistakes
- experimental recipes
- customer misuse
- illegal or restricted requests

Possible outcomes:

- spoiled batch
- weak potion
- unexpected side effect
- shop damage
- customer complaint
- medical or magical emergency
- town rumour
- inspection
- guilt, apology, compensation, or cover-up scene

Accidents should usually open drama rather than simply delete progress. The most
interesting question is often not "Did something go wrong?" but "What do you do
after it goes wrong?"

## Orders, Deadlines & Events

Longer-running commitments can tie shop mechanics into narrative structure.

Wishlist systems:

- **Commissions:** custom orders due on future days.
- **Standing orders:** recurring needs from farms, guards, healers, or inns.
- **Festivals:** predictable demand spikes and themed opportunities.
- **Weather:** affects foraging, foot traffic, illness, spoilage, and travel.
- **Inspections:** quality, legality, tax, or guild pressure.
- **Emergencies:** sudden illness, fire, curse outbreak, accident, or scandal.
- **Market days:** supply price shifts and rare buying opportunities.
- **Rent day:** a regular financial deadline that gives generosity a cost.

These systems should give the calendar a pulse. A day should feel different
because of what is approaching, not only because a random scene appeared.

## Information & Diagnosis

Potion work is partly interpretation.

Useful mechanics:

- **Ask questions:** learn more about a customer's symptoms, motives, budget, or
  risk.
- **Inspect clues:** read body language, magical residue, old labels, rumours, or
  inconsistencies.
- **Recommend alternatives:** sell a different potion, suggest rest, refer to a
  healer, ask for help, or refuse service.
- **Knowledge gaps:** unknown conditions can be treated cautiously, researched, or
  mishandled.
- **Privacy:** sensitive requests can be handled discreetly or become gossip.

Diagnosis mechanics help the shop feel more intimate than a vending machine. They
also let prudence and compassion become playable stances.

## Mechanical Integration With Aphebis

The implementation should translate shop actions into Aphebis state rather than
building a separate simulation island.

Examples:

- Selling below cost may decrease coins but increase goodwill.
- Refusing a dangerous potion may lose immediate profit but improve shop standing
  with cautious townsfolk.
- Brewing while exhausted may add fatigue and create a flawed potion fact.
- Helping a poor customer may set a story fact that unlocks gratitude later.
- Letting the apprentice handle a sale may raise confidence, trust, or fear
  depending on support and outcome.
- A public mistake may increase gossip heat and add a follow-up inspection scene.
- Paying rent may remove a debt scene but leave stock too low for tomorrow.

The system should prefer reusable helper effects and semantic property
definitions, while apps own the concrete scene text, characters, endings, and
bespoke dramatic beats.

## Prioritisation

For an early playable slice, the most valuable mechanics are probably:

1. Small potion catalogue with stock and simple quality.
2. Coins, rent pressure, and visible daily expenses.
3. Customer requests with price, refusal, charity, and partial-fit outcomes.
4. Basic brewing/restocking that trades time, fatigue, and ingredients.
5. Apprentice help with trust, confidence, and mistake handling.
6. Reputation channels: shop standing, goodwill, and gossip heat.
7. A few commissions or deadlines that carry across days.

Later expansions can add deeper recipe discovery, seasonal ingredients,
equipment upgrades, faction reactions, inspections, weather, festivals, and
specialisation paths.

## Design Test

A proposed shop mechanic belongs in the system if it helps produce at least one
of these questions:

- What can I afford?
- Who am I willing to disappoint?
- What am I teaching my apprentice?
- Do I protect the shop, the customer, the town, or myself?
- Is this risk worth the possible story it creates?
- What kind of shop are people starting to believe this is?

If a mechanic only asks the player to optimise a number in isolation, it probably
belongs lower in priority, or it needs a stronger narrative consequence.
