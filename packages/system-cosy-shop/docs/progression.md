# Cosy Shop Progression

Progression matters. Even in a narrative-first cosy shop game, players need the
pleasure of becoming more capable, seeing the shop change, unlocking new options,
and feeling that earlier effort has accumulated into a different life.

For `system-cosy-shop`, progression should not be only a ladder of bigger
numbers. It should be a set of visible, playable transformations:

- the shop becomes more capable and more recognizable
- the potion practice grows from basic cures into a meaningful craft
- relationships deepen, strain, recover, or close off
- the town changes how it treats the shop
- the apprentice becomes a different person under the player's care
- the player-character develops a reputation, style, and moral history
- the story opens new pressures because of what has already happened

The goal is not to reject conventional progression. Recipes, upgrades, income,
efficiency, quality, and unlocks are useful. The goal is to connect those forms
of progression to Aphebis's dramatic structure so that getting better at the shop
also changes what kinds of scenes can happen.

## Progression Principles

Good progression for this system should be:

- **Visible:** the player can see what has improved, opened, changed, or become
  possible.
- **Fictional:** progression has an in-world explanation: better tools, practice,
  trust, reputation, knowledge, relationships, or town support.
- **Consequential:** new capability creates new dilemmas, not just easier play.
- **Plural:** there are several ways to grow, and they imply different shopkeeper
  identities.
- **Stateful:** progression is tracked in Aphebis state so authored scenes can
  respond to it.
- **Recoverable:** some losses should hurt without ending the run; rebuilding
  trust, reputation, stock, or confidence can be satisfying progression too.

The strongest version of progression asks: "What can I do now that I could not do
before, and what new responsibility comes with that?"

## Shop Progression

The shop itself should visibly improve, specialize, and acquire history.

Possible avenues:

- **Facilities:** better brewing room, safer storage, customer seating, privacy
  screen, apprentice station, display window, cellar, warded shelves.
- **Capacity:** more storage slots, larger batches, more daily customers, more
  commission slots, better spoilage resistance.
- **Reliability:** fewer accidents, better quality floor, improved diagnosis,
  safer substitutions, cleaner records.
- **Atmosphere:** cosy, clinical, prestigious, mysterious, charitable, practical,
  experimental, or discreet.
- **Special services:** custom commissions, emergency callouts, discreet
  consultations, festival stalls, mail orders, guild work, training lessons.

Shop upgrades should not be neutral. A warm waiting area may attract vulnerable
customers. A polished storefront may attract wealthier clients and harsher public
judgement. A privacy room may bring secrets. A larger cauldron may tempt risky
batch brewing.

## Potion & Craft Progression

The potion practice should move from survival craft toward mastery.

Possible avenues:

- **Recipe unlocks:** learn new potion types through books, mentors, customers,
  experimentation, festivals, guilds, or story events.
- **Recipe mastery:** repeated brewing improves quality, reduces ingredient cost,
  lowers accident risk, or unlocks variants.
- **Technique growth:** stabilising, dilution, concentration, preservation,
  diagnosis, charmwork, distillation, antidote work, or side-effect management.
- **Ingredient knowledge:** identify substitutions, seasonal sources, ethical
  suppliers, spoilage signs, and rare reagent uses.
- **Signature style:** the shop becomes known for gentle remedies, precise cures,
  strong draughts, beautiful presentation, affordable help, experimental blends,
  or discreet work.
- **Experimental branches:** risky discoveries can open powerful recipes, unusual
  customers, inspections, or moral complications.

Potion progression should create interesting customer decisions. A stronger
sleep draught is not only better; it may be more dangerous, more profitable, more
regulated, or more tempting to misuse.

## Economic Progression

Money progression should produce relief, ambition, and new pressure.

Possible avenues:

- **Stability:** move from desperate rent pressure to modest breathing room.
- **Liquidity:** maintain enough coins to buy rare ingredients, absorb charity,
  or survive bad days.
- **Supplier access:** unlock better prices, reliable deliveries, rare stock, or
  ethically sourced ingredients.
- **Debt changes:** pay down old obligations, take risky loans, enter patronage,
  or owe favours instead of coins.
- **Revenue mix:** walk-in sales, commissions, standing orders, festival stalls,
  guild contracts, emergency fees, or teaching.
- **Cost profile:** repairs, taxes, wages, rent, supplier bills, storage
  improvements, and ingredient spoilage.

The player should feel financial improvement, but money should not erase drama.
Being comfortable should introduce different stories: status pressure, requests
for patronage, bigger orders, envy, inspection, and the fear of losing what has
been built.

## Reputation Progression

Reputation is progression through social memory.

Useful channels:

- **Shop standing:** the town believes the shop is competent, legitimate, and
  dependable.
- **Goodwill:** people believe the shop is kind, fair, and woven into community
  life.
- **Gossip heat:** the town is paying attention, for good or ill.
- **Specialisation:** the shop is known for a particular kind of service or
  moral posture.
- **Faction standing:** healers, farmers, nobles, workers, guards, suppliers,
  clergy, guilds, and rival shops may evaluate the same choices differently.
- **Trust radius:** more people bring private, urgent, or dangerous requests
  because of what they believe the shop can handle.

Reputation progression should change customer mix and scene availability. A shop
known for charity sees more desperate people. A shop known for discretion hears
more secrets. A shop known for dangerous potency attracts dangerous requests.

## Relationship Progression

Relationships should progress through remembered treatment, not abstract points
alone.

Possible avenues:

- **Trust:** characters believe the shopkeeper will keep promises or handle risk.
- **Affection:** warmth, fondness, loyalty, and emotional closeness.
- **Respect:** belief in competence, judgement, and seriousness.
- **Obligation:** favours owed, debts unpaid, promises made, or social leverage.
- **Resentment:** accumulated wounds, humiliation, exploitation, or neglect.
- **Fear:** compliance without safety, especially in apprentice and vulnerable
  customer relationships.
- **Familiarity:** repeated encounters make dialogue more intimate and choices
  more personal.

Relationship progression should unlock scenes, alter choices inside scenes, and
change consequences. A familiar customer may accept advice. A resentful one may
spread gossip. A trusting one may reveal the real problem. A fearful apprentice
may hide a mistake until it becomes costly.

## Apprentice Progression

The apprentice is one of the clearest progression mirrors in the system.

Possible avenues:

- **Skill:** brewing, sorting, diagnosis, customer handling, record keeping,
  safety, errands, or research.
- **Confidence:** willingness to try, speak, admit uncertainty, and act under
  pressure.
- **Trust:** belief that the shopkeeper will teach rather than merely punish.
- **Independence:** ability to run parts of the shop or make judgement calls.
- **Style:** careful, bold, inventive, compassionate, commercial, fearful,
  secretive, loyal, or ambitious.
- **Relationship arc:** assistant, student, burden, confidant, rival, successor,
  or found-family bond.

Apprentice progression should affect operations and story. A trained apprentice
can reduce fatigue or handle orders, but the deeper payoff is emotional: the
player sees what their leadership has made possible.

## Player-Character Progression

The shopkeeper should progress as a person, not only as an operator.

Possible axes:

- **Compassion:** how often the player chooses care when it costs something.
- **Prudence:** how often the player chooses caution, preparation, and restraint.
- **Ambition:** how strongly the player pursues growth, status, mastery, or
  influence.
- **Fatigue history:** whether the shopkeeper repeatedly overworks, recovers, or
  learns sustainable rhythms.
- **Moral pattern:** generous, exacting, secretive, opportunistic, protective,
  dutiful, reckless, community-minded, status-seeking.
- **Self-image:** the story can reflect what the shopkeeper seems to believe
  about who they are and what the shop is for.

This progression can be mostly semantic rather than class-based. The game does
not need to say "you are level 4 compassionate." It can say the town, apprentice,
and available scenes now treat the shopkeeper as someone with a history.

## Narrative Progression

Aphebis gives progression a strong narrative backbone.

Progress can be represented through:

- **Scene availability:** new scenes appear because of state, reputation,
  relationships, or unresolved facts.
- **Scene removal:** old pressures close when debts are paid, apologies are made,
  relationships break, or opportunities are missed.
- **Choice changes:** a scene can offer different options when the shop has the
  right potion, trust, reputation, fatigue level, or prior promise.
- **Outcome changes:** the same choice can land differently based on accumulated
  state.
- **Story facts:** important events become durable truth for later content.
- **Semantic labels:** state becomes readable in human terms like tired,
  rent-ready, trusted, volatile, or comfortable.
- **Endings:** final outcomes reflect patterns of business health, community
  standing, apprentice growth, and player identity.

This is where progression and narrative stop competing. Unlocks are not only
content gates; they are evidence that the story remembers who the player has
become.

## Calendar Progression

The calendar can make progression feel paced and seasonal.

Possible structures:

- **Rent cycles:** regular financial deadlines.
- **Weekly rhythms:** market day, supplier day, rest day, festival prep, guild
  visits, healer rounds.
- **Seasonal arcs:** different ingredients, illnesses, customers, festivals, and
  weather.
- **Event tracks:** a scandal grows, a festival approaches, a rival expands, a
  sickness spreads, a noble order looms.
- **Milestones:** first profitable day, first commission, first mistake handled,
  first rare recipe, first apprentice-led sale, first town crisis.

Calendar progression helps the player feel that the shop exists in time. It also
creates anticipation: tomorrow matters because something is coming.

## Progression Shapes

Different games built on this system may prefer different progression shapes.

Useful shapes:

- **Linear growth:** the shop steadily improves through early, mid, and late game
  capabilities.
- **Branching specialisation:** the shop becomes known for a particular style or
  clientele.
- **Reputation web:** different groups open and close opportunities based on
  behaviour.
- **Relationship arcs:** recurring characters progress through bespoke emotional
  routes.
- **Seasonal mastery:** the player learns the town's rhythms and prepares better
  each cycle.
- **Recovery arc:** the shop begins damaged, indebted, mistrusted, or exhausted
  and slowly heals.
- **Costed success:** the shop can flourish materially while losing warmth,
  trust, or safety.

The best progression model may combine several of these. A player might grow the
shop economically, specialize in discreet remedies, repair town trust, and raise
an apprentice who becomes bold enough to challenge them.

## Early Slice Priorities

For a first playable version, progression should be small but unmistakable.

High-value early progression:

1. **Recipe unlocks:** start with a few basic potions and unlock several more
   through scenes or repeated play.
2. **Shop upgrade:** add one or two visible improvements that change available
   actions or customer types.
3. **Apprentice growth:** let trust and confidence alter delegation, mistakes,
   and dialogue.
4. **Reputation movement:** shop standing, goodwill, and gossip heat should
   affect at least a few future scenes.
5. **Economic relief:** paying rent or building a coin buffer should feel
   meaningfully different from scraping by.
6. **Recurring customer memory:** at least one customer should return changed by
   prior treatment.
7. **Ending reflection:** the end state should name what kind of shop and
   shopkeeper emerged.

These pieces are enough to show the player that the game is not only a sequence
of good scenes. It is a life accumulating shape.

## Design Test

A progression mechanic is probably worth building if it answers at least one of
these questions:

- What new thing can I do now?
- Who treats me differently because of what I did?
- What has become easier, and what has become more complicated?
- What did the shop become known for?
- What did my apprentice learn from me?
- What future scene exists because of this progress?

Progression should make the player feel stronger, more rooted, and more
responsible all at once.
